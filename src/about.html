What is the 10 days?

The 10 Days was started in 2007 by three college students who wanted their friends to see that together they could change the world. Today, participants from across the United States come together to give up every beverage but water from Nov. 10 - Nov. 19th and donate what they save to providing Rwandans with clean water.

what is the goal?

The purpose of the 10 days is two fold:

1. Challenge students to think about how their choices matter.

2. Challenge students to think about how their choices matter.

why water?

Helping communities create sustainable water, sanitation, hygiene, and Christian witness programs in partnership with local churches is just the best way we've found to do that. Why? Because the water crisis affects poverty, women, health and education—and for us it's a spiritual issue.

Safety

Many women spend 20 hours per week collecting water, some walking 7 miles a day, often for contaminatted water. Too often the walk is not safe for them physically. It is women and the children they raise who suffer most without water, and who benefit most from access to it.

Water
=
Health

Water-related diseases cause 2.2 million deaths a year; every day, diarrhea takes the lives of 2,000 children in Africa—more than any other single cause of death. Safe water, a toilet, and clean hands could prevent 90% of these deaths.

Water
=
Education

Globally, water-related diseases steal 443 million school days a year. Roundworm, whipworm, and hookworm stunt growth, cause debilitating anemia, and follow kids into the classroom, shrinking cognitive potential. Illness causes absenteeism and early drop-out. Simple solutions can keep kids in school.

The Gospel

There's hope. In the past 20 years 2 billion people have gained access to safe drinking water—but that's not the best story we have to tell. For us water is a picture of living water—the good news that God wants to transform people, communities, and the whole world through the person of Jesus Christ.

783 million people lack access to an improved source of drinking water.

That's 1 in 9 of us. In the places we work, it's more like 1 in 2.

working in ruhango

The Ruhango District is located in the Southern Province of Rwanda. It’s about 242 square miles and is home to more than 300,000 people. All funds raised from the 10 Days go to funding Living Water International’s ongoing work in this area.

Meet Moses

Moses first started working for Living Water Kenya in 2005 and in 2009 he joined the Living Water Rwanda team as the Project Coordinator. Moses spends a lot of time in the field with the drilling team, managing each drilling site and over seeing the mechanics.

A typical day for Moses starts at 6 am as he gathers the team, ensures the mechanics on the drilling rig are functioning properly, and loads up the trucks for the days work.  Before a new borehole is drilled, he confirms that each drilling site scouted by the community engagement coordinator is accessible by the drilling rig truck.

As the head driller, Moses oversees the rest of the drilling team, making sure all equipment is being used properly and all safety precautions are practiced. After a borehole is completed, he completes the report for the new water source, often ending his day around 10 pm. Moses may work long days, but he loves his job and the work he gets to be a part of within the communities.
